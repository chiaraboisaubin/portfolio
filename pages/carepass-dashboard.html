Context

The project is to update the Carepass membership profile page and slowly add a space allowing users to access others' programs.

Initial goal

The goal of the business was to increase visits on the dashboard, reduce the amount of customer service calls that we sync the user interfaces membership page, and minimize the cancelation of membership. The design goals were to make the information easy to find on the membership page, organize the content, and sync the user interface with the new CVS UI kit and components while staying as close as possible to Carepass's current brand style and practice responsive and mobile-first design.

Competitive and UX analysis

I did competitive research on what other companies did for their membership or dashboard pages. The companies looked into were Walmart, Amazon, Target, and Adobe. I noticed the information was two pages with all the data. The cancel call to action was visible; they used certain information. Most profiles and memberships did have a hierarchy of information. Their style was simple, and they were able to add new containers. The content was a hierarchy of information about the membership. I also looked at how the companies showed how they organized multiple programs.
Baymard provided feedback on the CVS shopping experience that would help connect the UX e-commerce experience. Some of their callouts were to make sure that we have the ability for users to go back to the main page of content if they were navigated to another page and have information have some flow.
Looking at the current membership of Carepass, we noticed that getting to the dashboard page was not as accessible. The content was misleading or not as clear and repetitive content on the same page.

User testing

We worked with the research team to conduct moderate user testing with the existing page. We interviewed five participants who were current Carepass members and had done user testing in the past. We also looked for users who had a type of disability that they needed technical assistance. Our goals were to see their expectations regarding the dashboard, see if the participants could navigate to the dashboard page, and see their thoughts on the current information about Carepass on the page. With the data that was collected from the testing, we learned the following:
  1. 3 out of 5 participants suggested reorganizing the content and the information that needed clarification.
  2. 2 participants thought that the cancelation call to action should be more visible.
  3. 2 participants users said there was significant white space on the page.
  4. All participants identify seeing membership information as the top 3 important to them.
  5. All participants responded that they were satisfied with the current. Dashboard content.
Even though we conducted user testing and user interviews, we used the CVS persona Micheal to sync up with CVS to ensure we were going with other users' goals.

Wireframe and Final mockup

With the information that was gathered from the user testing, the goals of all specialties, and the CVS persona that already existed, me and other designers worked together to see how to make the information flow and not repeat information. The wireframe was also the space where we ensured that people who were a part of the project and other teams were aligned on how data would be presented.

At this time, Carepass was also introducing a new feature for gaining rewards with the amount of steps a users take. Leadership wanted to make the feature apart of the dashboard. We that additional information we needed the following experiences: New user, existing user with or without the reward healthy behavior and showing avaible monthly free $10 or the next monthly free $10, so about eight user scenarios.
Once we agreed on how we wanted the information, I started working on the user interface. I made sure that we were designing mobile-first, responsive design, implementing the CVS UI kit and components, and staying as close as possible to the Carepass brand. Mobile was less challenging than desktop, and there was some disagreement of the style for simplicity but ensuring that there was the ability to add more programs and features to the page. We also had to deal with accessibility calls out.
I was the only person at the time who knew how to use Figma, I connected most of the existing components to the UI kit, and build a prototype for the web experience.

Overview

The discussion and handoff were broken down by feature container so leadership and developers could look at each and make their development plan for building. There were a lot of challenges with this project like Figma was new to the team, a lot of different departments and people feedback that needed to stay with the project's goals, and the tight deadline. I believe we did achieve the goals of simplicity, making sure that we do best accessible and design practices and the ability to add information in the future. The following steps were to do an A/B testing with the saving container, update ways to navigate, and look for ways to improve the app experience.
