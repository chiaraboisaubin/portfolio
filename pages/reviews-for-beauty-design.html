Making beauty review page inclusive

Tools used

Context

CVS wanted to give the shopping experience a personal feel to the user. One of the ways was to customize the rating and review; looking at a specific environment for beauty experience, we saw an opportunity to make the experience use inclusive methods when asking people to describe their features when writing and reading reviews and on the product detail page, especially regarding skin complexion and hair. This project was an ongoing experience that I took over; after reviewing the documents and the prototype with the existing knowledge for the user to create or read rating and review, some places did leave people of color, people who had more than one complexion due to vertigo, high implementation, or even season changing. This was one of my favorite projects so far. Being a person of color, with locs, multiple pigmentations, and being a part of the Afro-Latino communication, this was an opportunity to get a large company more accessible and show that they are doing their best to grow into an inclusive platform.

Initial goal

The goals were to make more users use the rate and review within CVS, make the experience appreciate words and phrases that users prefer, and have an interface that gives users the flexibility to describe their physical features to work for each platform appropriately.

Desk research

Working with the user researcher on our team, we learned that there were different systems for identifying hair and skin tones/undertones. Many companies were transitioning from using notable names to Von Lushan's and Monk's scales to identify shades. Since CVS was not a company with its beauty cosmetics and is not known for beauty, for the competitive analysis, we wanted to find some companies that did sell beauty products and how they make the experience flexible for users to find that information. Looking at other companies, we were looking for a couple focused on hair products, foundations, skincare, and places to find all. Presenation on the existing system to idenfty hair and skin

Prototype

With the desk research and competitive analysis we gathered, I updated an existing high-fidelity prototype for rating. With the input of adding the reviews, I wanted the user to be able to choose more than one shade for their skin complexion and hair type. We also added bald, protective hairstyles, locs, and a space for others to include uncommon hair types. For the UI, we use pills because they are easy to use for mobile and web platforms, but we also wanted users to select multiple elements. When the user was reading the review, if they were looking at lipstick or foundation, they saw the color and name of the shade a current user purchased and focused on what they identified as their skin shade and location. For hair, we show how they will describe their hair. If the user wanted to filter the experience, we used the same pill experience for multi-selection. I also adjust the UI depending on the platform type they focus on so it functions to its native experience. After showing the work to my leads and stakeholders and there was limited feedback, we wanted to conduct an unmoderated user testing to see how users feel about the prototype and hear their shopping experience when looking for hair products and skin products for their complexion.

User testing

With the user testing, we had 10 participants. We had 3 out of 10 participants that identified as Cauascains, and the rest were either multi-racial, identified as black/African American, or other. When it came to finding a foundation, 7 of 10 participants said they had difficulty knowing what some companies meant with the names of the shades. They would find and stick with a product, using the ratings and reviews to help with their shopping experience. For hair products, a range of users found it easy to find products for their hair type, or there needed to be more clarity.
With the prototype, when it came to writing the reviews, all the users did not know that they could choose more than one shade or hair type. However, when filtering to look for other studies, they were fine. Some users' feedback was to have a note to let them know that there was the ability to choose one over one; others did prefer checkboxes (we did look into checkboxes, but on a mobile experience, this was not an accessible, friendly experience compared to using pills. Also, checkboxes are not iOS-friendly because of the new method that Apple has used). 8 out of 10 participants liked the approach we were going to and talked about their frustrations about not feeling like they were being included. The other users did not think it was an issue or did not think about it; this seemed to show by the different age groups than racial backgrounds, With the information we gathered from the user testing, I adjusted the design and collaborated with our content designer to clarify the instructions. Even though we got some viable information from the user testing, we have yet to hear from other ethnic backgrounds or have the numbers to see if we were going in the right direction for this. The steps were to use a third-party organization that gathered data from other companies and surveys to learn more about how people prefer how they want to describe themselves, how they feel, if the shopping experience was inclusive or going in a direction that allows the user.

Overview

The development goal was to do web and native experience simultaneously, but the web will launch so we could test with the accessibility team. This is an ongoing project that we also work with another company to collect surveys from users to see if a particular company was being inclusive and how users felt about their shopping experience - if they felt isolated or felt like they were being thought about their culture or ethnicity. The following steps were to look at the product detail page and how we can make that inclusive and use existing hair and skin tone systems to help users with shopping. This project also connected with a project I did for the filtering on the product list page for natives; it also was a way to introduce how to make the product detail page to make the information clear and find ways to assist with users' shopping experience.
